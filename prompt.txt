--- BEGIN APPROVED ENGLISH ABSTRACT ---

Irregularly sampled intensive care unit records contain measurements collected at different times and frequencies, with missingness patterns that may reflect both the patient’s condition and the clinical process. This thesis develops and evaluates a computational framework that combines design-time knowledge elicitation assisted by a large language model, deterministic construction of clinically inspired proxy states, prediction of those states from irregular medical time series, and directed-acyclic-graph-guided observational analyses of in-hospital mortality. The objective is to examine whether these stages can be integrated into a transparent and traceable analytical workflow, rather than to validate a clinical decision system or recommend treatment.


For the primary original-cohort CausalForestDML analyses, the mean model-estimated effect was positive for all nine proxy states examined in MIMIC-III. In PhysioNet, nine of ten estimates were positive, while the estimate for shock was negative. CausalForestDML and LinearDML agreed in direction in all 19 dataset–proxy-state comparisons. All three estimators agreed in 18 of 19 comparisons; CausalPFN was retained as an exploratory estimator because its available methodological provenance and diagnostic support were more limited. Matching analyses provided descriptive comparisons between matched records and were not interpreted as evidence of an intervention effect. Results were reported separately for the two datasets and were not pooled.

The main contribution of this work is an integrated and inspectable workflow that connects language-model-assisted design proposals with source-encoded rules and graphs, and then follows the resulting proxy constructs through prediction and observational analysis. The estimates remain conditional on the validity of the proxy definitions, the correctness of the assumed graphs and temporal ordering, adequate control of measured confounding, sufficient overlap, model assumptions, and the absence of important unmeasured confounding. Consequently, the findings do not establish that the proxy states caused mortality or that modifying them would change clinical outcomes. Clinical review, external validation, stronger causal designs, and more complete computational provenance remain necessary.

--- END APPROVED ENGLISH ABSTRACT ---
