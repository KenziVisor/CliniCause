# 7. Section 1 — Motivation: Irregular ICU Time-Series Analysis

Develop a focused motivation moving from clinical data characteristics to the thesis problem.

## 7.1 Irregular ICU data

Explain that ICU electronic health records contain:

* measurements taken at nonuniform times;
* variables measured at different frequencies;
* substantial missingness;
* changes in measurement intensity driven partly by clinical processes;
* heterogeneous patient and care trajectories;
* high-dimensional physiological and administrative information.

State that irregularity and missingness are not merely formatting inconveniences.

They can affect:

* representation learning;
* prediction;
* cohort construction;
* proxy-state assignment;
* causal adjustment;
* interpretation.

Use appropriate literature on irregular medical time series and informative missingness.

Possible keys include:

```text
sun_2026_review_irregular_medical_timeseries
lipton_kale_wetzel_2016_missingness_rnns
che2018grud
tipirneni2022strats
```

Do not claim that any cited paper validates the local preprocessing or local results.

## 7.2 Two complementary needs

Explain that useful analysis requires both:

1. representations capable of handling irregular longitudinal measurements;
2. clinically interpretable analytical variables that can connect prediction to later causal questions.

Introduce proxy states as the thesis’s chosen intermediate representation:

```text
clinically inspired, rule-derived analytical constructs
```

State immediately that they are not:

```text
chart-adjudicated diagnoses
ground truth
validated phenotypes
```

## 7.3 Prediction and causal analysis are different tasks

Explain the distinction between:

* predicting rule-derived proxy labels;
* estimating adjusted mortality contrasts for proxy-state exposures.

High prediction performance does not automatically establish:

* clinical validity of the labels;
* causal validity;
* treatment actionability.

Introduce the need for:

* explicit causal assumptions;
* project-specified DAGs;
* adjustment logic;
* support diagnostics;
* sensitivity analysis;
* transparent provenance.

## 7.4 Dataset context

Introduce:

```text
PhysioNet 2012
MIMIC-III
```

at a high level.

State that both contain ICU time-series data but differ in:

* size;
* era;
* variables;
* data contracts;
* measurement processes;
* proxy-state definitions;
* causal graphs.

Do not provide unverified raw cohort totals.

The Introduction may state that the completed original causal-analysis populations differed substantially, but reserve exact counts for the brief findings preview or Chapter 10.

# 8. Section 2 — Thesis Gap and Objective

## 8.1 Gap

Frame the research gap as an integration gap.

Existing research provides important work on:

* irregular-time-series representation;
* missingness-aware prediction;
* electronic phenotyping;
* weak supervision and label aggregation;
* DAG-guided causal analysis;
* double machine learning;
* heterogeneous-effect estimation;
* sensitivity and overlap diagnostics.

However, these components are often studied separately.

The thesis addresses the practical and methodological challenge of connecting them in a single auditable workflow.

The gap is not that no previous work exists in any component.

Avoid claims such as:

```text
first ever
entirely novel
no prior work
```

unless directly supported by a formal systematic review, which this thesis does not contain.

## 8.2 Objective

State the objective clearly:

```text
To construct and evaluate an evidence-tracked framework that converts
irregular ICU measurement data into clinically inspired proxy-state
representations and uses those representations, under explicit causal
assumptions, for DAG-guided adjusted effect estimation of in-hospital
mortality.
```

Explain the implemented sequence:

```text
dataset-specific preprocessing
→ rule-based proxy-state construction
→ multi-label proxy-state prediction
→ prediction normalization and algorithmic aggregation
→ project-specified DAGs
→ exposure-specific adjustment
→ matching and heterogeneous-effect estimation
→ robustness and provenance evaluation
```

## 8.3 LLM-assisted design role

State transparently that:

* LLM prompts contributed candidate clinical and causal design material;
* source code defines the implemented rules and DAGs;
* the LLM was not an executed component of the analysis pipeline;
* the LLM did not learn the DAG from data;
* LLM output was not clinical validation.

Do not foreground the LLM as the thesis’s main contribution.

## 8.4 Scope boundaries

State that the thesis does not claim:

* validated clinical diagnoses;
* a learned causal graph;
* unconditional causal effects;
* well-defined treatment interventions for every proxy state;
* clinical recommendations;
* deployment readiness;
* full clean-checkout computational reproducibility.

A concise scope statement should distinguish:

```text
methodological and engineering contribution
empirical evaluation
clinical and causal limitations
```

## 9.2 Secondary research questions

```text
SRQ-1:
Which data contracts are needed to connect PhysioNet 2012, MIMIC-III,
the irregular-time-series prediction workflow, and the causal-analysis
pipeline?

SRQ-2:
Can rule-based proxy states be constructed consistently enough to serve
as prediction labels and causal-pipeline inputs?

SRQ-3:
How do STraTS and the included sequence-model baselines perform on
multi-label proxy-state prediction across MIMIC-III and PhysioNet?

SRQ-4:
How are predicted proxy states normalized and aggregated before
downstream analysis?

SRQ-5:
What observed adjustment sets are selected by the dataset-specific,
project-specified DAGs for proxy-state exposure analyses?

SRQ-6:

evidence is available for the prespecified proxy-state exposures?

SRQ-7:
What limitations follow from proxy labels, intervention definition,
overlap, measurement error, model dependence, provenance, and
unmeasured confounding?
```

The wording may be polished for parallel structure, but the substance and numbering must match Chapter 11.

Do not introduce a new research question.

## 9.3 Contribution hierarchy

Present contributions in descending order of importance.

### Primary contribution

An end-to-end, evidence-tracked research framework linking:

* irregular ICU preprocessing;
* proxy-state construction;
* proxy-state prediction;
* algorithmic aggregation;
* DAG-guided adjustment;
* matching;
* heterogeneous-effect estimation;
* robustness diagnostics.

### Secondary methodological contributions

Include:

1. A shared proxy-state layer serving as both a prediction target and a downstream structured exposure representation.
2. Explicit dataset-specific data-contract handling connecting the predictive and causal repositories.
3. Source-coded, exposure-specific DAG adjustment logic with traceable LLM-assisted design provenance.
4. Multi-estimator analysis using CausalForestDML, LinearDML, and exploratory CausalPFN.
5. Integrated matching, sensitivity, permutation, and support diagnostics.

### Empirical contributions

Include:

1. A checked comparison of five predictive model families across two ICU datasets.
2. Original-cohort adjusted-effect analyses for every prespecified dataset-specific proxy-state exposure.
3. Cross-estimator directional comparison.
4. Separate robustness analyses under outcome-based downsampling.
5. Cross-dataset evaluation without numerical pooling.

### Reproducibility and evidence contribution
