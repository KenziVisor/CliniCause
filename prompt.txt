# 6. Create the conclusion evidence brief

Create:

```text
thesis-writing/logs/stage_4_10A_conclusion_evidence_brief.md
```

The brief must be created before drafting Chapter 12.

Use the following structure.

## 6.1 Research problem

State the problem addressed by the thesis in no more than one concise paragraph.

Distinguish:

* irregular clinical time-series representation;
* proxy-state construction and prediction;
* downstream observational causal analysis;
* the integration and provenance problem connecting these stages.

## 6.2 Supported workflow summary

List the actual workflow components represented in the thesis:

* dataset preprocessing;
* project-specific clinical proxy-state construction;
* predictive modeling of proxy states;
* prediction normalization or aggregation where applicable;
* clinician- or project-authored causal graph use;
* matching-based descriptive summaries;
* CausalForestDML;
* LinearDML;
* exploratory CausalPFN analysis;
* overlap, sensitivity, and permutation diagnostics where actually available.

Do not describe an implemented component as executed unless the thesis evidence establishes execution.

## 6.3 Supported contribution set

For each proposed contribution, record:

```text
Contribution
Evidence source
Permitted wording
Forbidden stronger wording
Confidence: supported / supported with qualification / exploratory / blocked
```

Potential contribution classes include:

* integrated pipeline design;
* common proxy-state interface between predictive and causal stages;
* cross-dataset application;
* comparative predictive evaluation;
* transparent rule-based proxy construction;
* DAG-guided adjustment workflow;
* heterogeneous-effect estimation;
* estimator agreement and disagreement analysis;
* evidence and provenance tracking.

Do not invent novelty.

Do not claim that the thesis invented the underlying time-series models, DML, causal forests, weak supervision, DAGs, matching, or sensitivity methods.

## 6.4 Supported findings

Extract only findings already reported in Chapter 10 and interpreted in Chapter 11.

For every finding, record:

```text
Finding
Exact source location
Whether a number may be repeated
Required qualification
```

Prefer a small set of central findings over a long catalogue of values.

Check every repeated number directly against Chapter 10.

## 6.5 Exploratory or bounded findings

Explicitly identify findings that cannot be presented as primary conclusions, including:

* CausalPFN findings;
* findings with incomplete diagnostic support;
* treatment or dataset comparisons with poor matching support;
* estimates showing material estimator disagreement;
* findings dependent on proxy-state validity;
* downsampled-cohort robustness results;
* findings with unresolved provenance limitations.

## 6.6 Required limitations

At minimum, evaluate whether the conclusion must retain:

* observational identification assumptions;
* unmeasured confounding;
* overlap and empirical-support limitations;
* project-specific proxy-state validity;
* proxy-label and prediction error propagation;
* uncertainty in the DAG;
* estimator disagreement;
* incomplete CausalPFN diagnostics and literature support;
* missing clinical review;
* data and artifact provenance limitations;
* external validity;
* absence of clinical deployment validation;
* LLM-assisted design provenance limitations.

## 6.7 Permitted future work

Every future-work item must connect to a documented limitation or an uncompleted validation requirement.

For each item, record:

```text
Future-work item
Limitation addressed
Concrete next research action
Whether it requires new data, expert review, implementation, or experimentation
```

## 6.8 Forbidden conclusion claims

Create an explicit list of statements the chapter must not make.

At minimum prohibit claims that:

* the proxy states are verified diagnoses;
* the project DAG is clinically validated;
* estimator use proves causality;
* association, matching, or directional agreement proves a treatment effect;
* CausalPFN has the same theoretical or diagnostic support as the DML estimators;
* the models are ready for clinical deployment;
* an LLM served as a medical expert or causal-discovery method;
* the study established individualized treatment recommendations;
* overlap or positivity was proven globally;
* all experiments are reproducible from the tracked repository alone;
* the thesis invented the cited models or causal estimators.

# 8. Content requirements

## 8.1 Summary of the study

Summarize:

* the motivating problem;
* why irregular ICU time series and proxy clinical states matter;
* how the predictive and causal components were connected;
* the datasets and broad model families;
* the role of evidence tracking and conservative interpretation.

Do not repeat implementation-level detail.

## 8.2 Findings and contributions

Present the contributions in descending order of support.

Clearly distinguish:

* engineering or workflow contributions;
* methodological application contributions;
* empirical findings;
* exploratory findings.

Maintain the approved hierarchy:

* original cohorts are primary;
* outcome-downsampled analyses are robustness analyses;
* CausalForestDML is primary;
* LinearDML is secondary;
* CausalPFN is exploratory;
* matching is a descriptive and empirical-support baseline, not independent causal proof.

Use numerical values sparingly.

Any number included must be copied exactly from Chapter 10 and checked against its table or paragraph source.

## 8.3 Limitations

Do not replace limitations with generic statements.

Focus on limitations that change interpretation of the findings.

State clearly that:

* proxy states are project-specific analytical constructs;
* causal estimates depend on stated assumptions;
* diagnostics reduce but do not eliminate uncertainty;
* estimator agreement is informative but not proof;
* disagreement is substantive evidence of fragility;
* clinical utility and deployment were not validated.

## 8.4 Future work

Future work should be prioritized and concrete.

Possible categories, only where supported by the evidence brief:

1. clinical review and validation of proxy-state definitions;
2. external and temporal validation on additional cohorts;
3. improved provenance and fully reproducible artifact manifests;
4. prospective or target-trial-aligned study designs;
5. stronger overlap handling and support-aware estimands;
6. richer uncertainty and sensitivity analysis;
7. investigation of measurement and label error propagation;
8. improved evaluation of predicted versus rule-derived proxy states;
9. verified theoretical and diagnostic treatment of CausalPFN;
10. clinically meaningful effect-modifier and subgroup validation;
11. ablations isolating individual pipeline handoffs;
12. human-governed documentation of LLM-assisted design decisions.

Do not present planned work as completed.

## 8.5 Closing perspective

End with a measured statement about what the thesis demonstrates.

The final paragraph should emphasize the value of transparent integration and evidence boundaries, not clinical readiness or definitive causal discovery.

# 10. Numerical and causal-language protections

Before writing any number:

1. locate it in Chapter 10;
2. verify its dataset, cohort, exposure, estimator, and metric;
3. verify whether it is primary, secondary, exploratory, or robustness-only;
4. preserve its sign, denominator, rounding, and qualification.

Do not calculate a new aggregate.

Do not average findings across datasets or estimators unless Chapter 10 already reports that aggregate.

Avoid language such as:

```text
proved
demonstrated causally
clinically effective
protective
harmful
treatment recommendation
diagnosed
validated phenotype
clinically actionable
```

unless the exact local sentence explicitly rejects or qualifies that interpretation.

Prefer:

```text
estimated
was associated with
was directionally consistent
under the stated assumptions
within the observed analytical sample
project-specific proxy state
exploratory finding
supports further investigation
```
