\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Irregular intensive-care-unit (ICU) records contain nonuniformly timed, variably available measurements whose missingness can itself be informative. This thesis evaluates an evidence-tracked framework that combines LLM-assisted clinical and causal knowledge elicitation, deterministic proxy-label construction, deep proxy-state prediction from irregular ICU measurements, and, under explicit observational assumptions, DAG-guided adjusted analyses of in-hospital mortality. The aim is methodological integration with traceable analytical handoffs, not clinical validation, treatment recommendation, or deployment.

The workflow is evaluated separately in the PhysioNet 2012 and MIMIC-III cohorts. At design time, a structured LLM-assisted elicitation protocol produced proposals for the dataset-specific proxy-state ontologies, decision-rule families, missingness considerations, and directed acyclic graphs (DAGs); project-selected designs were then encoded as deterministic source code. Five deep time-series model families were trained to predict decision-tree-generated rule labels. The archived final causal analyses used the five-source aggregate: one rule-derived source plus GRU, GRU-D, STraTS, and TCN predicted-label sources. The LLM was not a runtime estimator. Proxy states are not chart-adjudicated diagnoses, and the project DAGs are neither learned from patient data nor clinically validated.

For the primary original-cohort CausalForestDML summaries, all nine MIMIC-III proxy-state summaries were positive; nine of ten PhysioNet summaries were positive, with shock negative. CausalForestDML and LinearDML agreed in direction in all 19 original-cohort dataset--exposure comparisons. All three estimators agreed in direction in 18 of 19 comparisons; CausalPFN is retained only as an exploratory estimator because its primary methodological source and comparable diagnostic support remain unresolved. Matching provides descriptive matched-pair outcome differences rather than confirmation of an intervention effect. Results are reported separately by dataset and are not pooled.

The contribution is a transparent framework that traces LLM-assisted proposals into source-encoded rules and graphs, then follows rule-derived constructs through prediction and observational analysis, with checked numerical artifacts and explicit provenance boundaries. The estimates remain conditional on proxy validity, graph correctness, temporal ordering, measured-confounding adequacy, support and overlap, model assumptions, and possible unmeasured confounding. The thesis establishes neither causation nor clinical readiness. Evidence records improve numerical traceability, not clean-checkout reproducibility. Further work requires clinical review, stronger designs, external validation, and complete provenance.
